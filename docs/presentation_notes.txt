=============================================================================
MPMC QUEUE BENCHMARK – PRESENTATION NOTES (Final Findings)
=============================================================================

Slide 1: Title
- Introduce the goal: compare MPMC queue implementations under controlled CPU scaling.
- Emphasise we use cgroups to remove hardware scaling, and LTTng for accurate timing.
- Hardware: laptop Ryzen 8845HS, power‑saving mode to avoid throttling.

Slide 2: Test Environment
- cgroups CPU limits: 1,2,4,8,15 cores.
- Fixed 16 producers, 16 consumers (thread count constant).
- Items per producer: 100k (steady state, not too long).
- Capacities: 1k (tight) and 100k (loose) – tests backpressure.
- Ratios: symmetric (1:1,4:4,8:8,16:16) and asymmetric (1:4,1:10,4:1,10:1).
- 3 runs per config, LTTng‑UST tracepoints, analysis with babeltrace.

Slide 3: Queues Under Test
- Four queues: mutex (blocking), ring buffer (lock‑free bounded), bounded MPMC (lock‑free bounded), hazard (unbounded lock‑free with hazard pointers).
- Hazard is the only unbounded one; uses hazard pointers for memory reclamation.

Slide 4: Throughput at 15 Cores (loose capacity)
- RingBuffer and BoundedMPMC achieve ~1.1 M ops/sec, 40% higher than mutex.
- Hazard is slightly behind mutex (0.78 vs 0.80).
- Insight: bounded lock‑free wins.

Slide 5: Scalability (speedup vs 1 core)
- RingBuffer and Bounded scale near‑linearly (15x at 15 cores).
- Mutex scales surprisingly well (13x) – low contention due to large capacity.
- Hazard stalls after 8 cores (only 6x at 15 cores).
- Key takeaway: hazard pointer overhead kills scalability.

Slide 6: Latency at 15 Cores (P99)
- RingBuffer and Bounded: ~8–9 µs enqueue+dequeue.
- Mutex: ~30 µs both.
- Hazard: enqueue 7.8 µs (excellent), dequeue 103 µs (terrible).
- Dequeue latency is the main problem.

Slide 7: Capacity Sensitivity
- Loose capacity (100k) → low contention → mutex performs well.
- Tight capacity (1k) → high contention → lock‑free shows bigger advantage.
- Recommendation: tune capacity to workload.

Slide 8: Hazard Queue Specific Failures
- Dequeue latency spikes, poor scaling, deadlocks under heavy CPU pressure.
- Hazard pointer scans become expensive.
- Conclusion: correct but not performant for this workload.

Slide 9: Mutex – Unexpectedly Competitive
- Throughput only 20‑30% lower than lock‑free.
- Scales well because of large capacity and adaptive spinning.
- Mutex is “good enough” for many real workloads.

Slide 10: RingBuffer vs BoundedMPMC – Tie
- Nearly identical performance.
- Both production‑ready.

Slide 11: Engineering Insights
- Table of observations and implications.
- cgroups essential for algorithmic tests.
- Capacity tuning matters.

Slide 12: What We Learned
- Lock‑free is not always better.
- Bounded queues are simpler and faster.
- Latency percentiles matter.
- Recommendation: start with bounded lock‑free.

Slide 13: Raw Data Example
- Show sample CSV.
- Reproducible.

Slide 14: Summary
- Best: RingBuffer / BoundedMPMC.
- Mutex viable under low contention.
- Hazard pointer queue not recommended for high throughput.
- Methodology (cgroups + LTTng) is key.

Time: ~20 minutes presentation + 10 minutes Q&A.
