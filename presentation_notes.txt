================================================================================
MPMC QUEUE BENCHMARK – PRESENTATION NOTES (Professional Findings Report)
================================================================================

Slide 1: Title
- Introduce the goal: compare MPMC queue implementations under controlled CPU scaling.
- Emphasise that we measure algorithmic scaling, not hardware scaling (cgroups).

Slide 2: Test Environment
- Hardware: 8C/16T, Linux, cgroups v2.
- Fixed load: 16P/16C, 500k items, capacity 100k (except hazard).
- Retries and averaging for statistical significance.

Slide 3: Queues Under Test
- Four implementations, uniform API.
- Hazard queue is the only unbounded one (Michael‑Scott + hazard pointers).
- All others are bounded.

Slide 4: Throughput at 15 Cores
- Ring buffer and bounded MPMC lead by ~40% over mutex.
- Hazard is slightly behind mutex.
- Insight: bounded lock‑free wins.

Slide 5: Scalability (Speedup vs Cores)
- Ring buffer and bounded scale near‑linearly (15x at 15 cores).
- Mutex also scales well (13x) – low contention due to large capacity.
- Hazard stalls after 8 cores (only 6x at 15 cores).
- Key takeaway: hazard pointer overhead kills scalability.

Slide 6: Latency at 15 Cores (P99)
- Ring buffer and bounded: ~8–9 µs enqueue+dequeue.
- Mutex: ~30 µs both.
- Hazard: enqueue 7.8 µs (excellent), dequeue 103 µs (terrible).
- Dequeue latency is the main problem.

Slide 7: Hazard Queue Specific Failures
- Dequeue latency spikes, poor scaling, deadlocks under heavy CPU pressure.
- Hazard pointer scans become expensive.
- Conclusion: correct but not performant for this workload.

Slide 8: Mutex – Unexpectedly Competitive
- Throughput only 20–30% lower than lock‑free.
- Scales well because of large capacity (low contention) and adaptive spinning.
- Mutex is “good enough” for many real workloads.

Slide 9: RingBuffer vs BoundedMPMC – Tie
- Nearly identical performance.
- Both production‑ready.

Slide 10: Engineering Insights
- Table of observations and implications.
- cgroups essential for algorithmic tests.
- Capacity tuning matters.

Slide 11: What We Learned
- Lock‑free is not always better.
- Bounded queues are simpler and faster.
- Latency percentiles matter.
- Recommendation: start with bounded lock‑free.

Slide 12: Raw Data Example
- Show sample CSV.
- Reproducible.

Slide 13: Summary
- Best: RingBuffer / BoundedMPMC.
- Mutex viable under low contention.
- Hazard pointer queue not recommended for high throughput.
- Methodology is key.

Time: ~20 minutes presentation + 10 minutes Q&A.
